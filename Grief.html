Understanding the difference

Normal grief vs. Prolonged Grief Disorder

Normal grief Prolonged Grief Disorder
Duration Gradually softens over months to a year Remains at acute intensity past 12 months
Daily functioning Mostly intact, with difficult periods Significantly impaired
Positive memories Can be accessed alongside sadness Inaccessible or bring only pain

Does time alone help Yes, for most No. Targeted treatment is needed.

The protocol

Evidence-based. Not just supportive listening.

The Columbia PGD protocol addresses the neurobiological mechanism keeping grief stuck. It is a structured course of treatment, not open-ended support.

01

Assessment & diagnosis

We establish whether grief meets PGD criteria, review medical records with our physician consultant, and set individualized treatment goals.

02

Grief monitoring

Structured tracking of grief intensity and daily functioning creates a clear baseline and makes progress visible over the course of treatment.

03

Loss focus & restoration

Alternating between loss-focused work (processing the reality of the death) and restoration focus (rebuilding identity and life goals) breaks the pattern that keeps grief stuck.

Questions

Good to know

What is Prolonged Grief Disorder?

Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) is a DSM-5-TR diagnosis characterized by grief that persists at acute, distressing intensity for more than 12 months following a loss, with significant impairment in daily functioning. It affects roughly 10% of bereaved people and does not resolve with time or willpower alone.

How is Prolonged Grief Disorder different from normal grief?

Normal grief gradually softens over months to a year or more, comes in waves, and allows the person to function and experience moments of joy. Prolonged Grief Disorder remains at acute intensity past 12 months with little softening, significantly impairs daily functioning, and leaves the person unable to access positive memories without overwhelming pain.

What is the treatment for Prolonged Grief Disorder?

The evidence-based treatment is the Prolonged Grief Disorder therapy protocol developed at Columbia University’s Center for Complicated Grief by Dr. Katherine Shear. It has a 70% efficacy rate and works by addressing the neurobiological mechanism keeping grief stuck, not by offering support while grief runs its course.

Is this therapy available via telehealth?

Yes. Prolonged Grief Disorder therapy is available via secure video throughout Texas. The protocol adapts fully to telehealth and does not require in-person attendance.
