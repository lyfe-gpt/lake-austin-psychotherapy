Getting Started

What types of therapy does Lake Austin Psychotherapy offer?

Lake Austin Psychotherapy offers EMDR therapy, physician-administered ketamine-assisted psychotherapy (KAP), Prolonged Grief Disorder therapy using Columbia University’s protocol, alcohol use disorder treatment, somatic-based therapy, mindfulness approaches, and talk therapy, tailored for anxiety, depression, trauma, PTSD, grief, and life transitions. EMDR and grief therapy are available via telehealth throughout Texas; KAP is in-person in Austin with private transportation available from Dallas, Houston, and San Antonio.

How do I know which service is right for me?

The free 1-hour consultation is designed for exactly this. A clinician will talk through your history, goals, and what you’ve already tried, then recommend the best approach. Sometimes one modality, sometimes a combination. EMDR and KAP can complement each other, and the team helps you understand where to start. No cost, no obligation.

What should I expect during my first free consultation?

A warm, judgment-free 1-hour Zoom conversation at no cost. You’ll discuss what brings you in, ask any questions about modalities, and hear a recommendation on fit and approach. The clinician will also explain pricing, the sliding scale, and provide a Good Faith Estimate as required by the No Surprises Act. No commitment required.

How quickly can I be seen?

There is no waitlist. Most clients begin within days of their consultation. The team responds to all inquiries seven days a week by call, email, or text.

Is therapy right for me?

Therapy is well-suited for anyone feeling overwhelmed, anxious, or stuck — or simply curious about growth. It provides an objective, confidential space for processing trauma, grief, life transitions, relationship challenges, and personal goals. You don’t need to be in crisis. Many clients begin therapy as a proactive investment in their wellbeing.

Do you work with adolescents, couples, or families?

Yes. The practice serves adolescents (16+), adults, couples, and families. Arlee Pryor, LMFT specializes in couples therapy using the Gottman Method, Relational Life Therapy (RLT), Prepare/Enrich premarital counseling, and CSTIP sex therapy. Several clinicians also specialize in adolescent care.

Is Lake Austin Psychotherapy LGBTQ-affirming?

Yes, explicitly. All clinicians are committed to affirming, non-judgmental care for clients of all sexual orientations, gender identities, personal beliefs, and religious affiliations.

What is the best therapy for PTSD in Austin, TX?

EMDR therapy is the gold-standard, evidence-based treatment for PTSD, recommended by the WHO, APA, and U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. For treatment-resistant PTSD that has not responded to standard therapy or medication, ketamine-assisted psychotherapy offers rapid, clinically validated relief. Lake Austin Psychotherapy provides both, delivered by an EMDRIA Certified therapist and a board-certified physician in Austin, with telehealth EMDR available throughout Texas.

How do I find a qualified EMDR therapist in Austin?

Look for EMDRIA certification, not just “EMDR-trained.” Basic EMDR training is a weekend workshop. EMDRIA Certified Therapist status requires completing 50+ EMDR sessions with 25+ clients, 20+ hours of supervised consultation with an EMDRIA Approved Consultant, and ongoing continuing education. The EMDRIA therapist directory at emdria.org lists certified providers by location. Mary K. Moore at Lake Austin Psychotherapy holds full EMDRIA certification and offers sessions in Austin and remotely throughout Texas.

Is telehealth therapy as effective as in-person sessions?

Yes. Multiple peer-reviewed meta-analyses confirm telehealth therapy achieves outcomes equivalent to in-person care for anxiety, depression, PTSD, and grief. EMDR has been specifically validated for remote delivery with the same efficacy rates as office sessions. The therapeutic relationship, not the physical room, drives outcomes.

EMDR Therapy

How can EMDR therapy help with trauma, PTSD, anxiety, and depression?

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) is a powerful, evidence-based therapy that helps the brain process and heal from trauma using bilateral stimulation — typically guided eye movements. It reduces the emotional charge of painful memories without requiring you to retell them in detail. The WHO, APA, and U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs all recommend EMDR for PTSD. It also effectively treats anxiety, depression, grief, addiction, and some somatic conditions like chronic pain. Remote EMDR sessions are available throughout Texas.

Do I have to retell my trauma in detail during EMDR?

No. EMDR processes the traumatic event itself rather than a verbal narrative of it. You do not need to describe what happened in detail. The processing is largely internal, guided by bilateral stimulation. Many clients find this a significant relief compared to traditional talk therapy approaches.

How is EMDR therapy at Lake Austin Psychotherapy different from other providers?

Mary K. Moore holds full EMDRIA certification — the highest credential in EMDR practice — requiring 50+ sessions with 25+ clients and 20+ hours of consultation beyond basic training. “EMDR-trained” (a standard weekend course) is a much lower standard. EMDRIA certification means the clinical approach is rigorously validated, not self-taught. Sessions are available remotely throughout Texas.

How many EMDR sessions will I need?

A single-incident trauma (car accident, assault) may resolve in 3–8 EMDR sessions. Complex or childhood trauma typically takes longer. One of EMDR’s primary advantages is speed — it generally resolves trauma faster than traditional talk therapy, often in months rather than years. The number of sessions is discussed after your free consultation once your history is understood.

Is EMDR therapy safe?

Yes. EMDR has been in clinical use since the late 1980s and is endorsed by the WHO, APA, and U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. It is non-invasive, requires no medication, and carries no medical side effects. Some clients experience temporary emotional intensity during processing as memories surface, which the therapist actively manages in session. EMDR is appropriate for most adults, including those who have not responded to other therapies.

How is EMDR therapy different from traditional talk therapy?

Talk therapy processes trauma through language — you describe what happened, examine how it affects you, and build insight over time. EMDR bypasses the narrative and works at the neurological level, using bilateral stimulation to help the brain reprocess the memory itself. You do not need to describe what happened in detail. The result: EMDR typically resolves trauma faster than talk therapy, often in months rather than years, and many clients find it less emotionally exhausting.

Ketamine-Assisted Psychotherapy

Is ketamine therapy effective for treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, and anxiety?

Yes. Randomized controlled trials show ketamine-assisted therapy achieves 67–71% response rates for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD (American Journal of Psychiatry, 2021). At Lake Austin Psychotherapy, over 95% of KAP clients report sustained relief for months following a single infusion paired with integration therapy. KAP provides rapid relief, often within hours of infusion, while EMDR typically requires several weekly sessions. Both can be used together.

How is Lake Austin Psychotherapy’s ketamine approach different from other clinics?

Three key differences: (1) Physician at bedside. Dr. Truman Milling Jr., MD, FACEP is a board-certified emergency physician and NIH-funded researcher present for the entire infusion. Most clinics do not have a physician present throughout. (2) Single-infusion model. One carefully administered infusion plus ongoing integration therapy, not a package of six. The goal is permanent rewiring, not recurring revenue. (3) Integrated psychotherapy. Weekly post-infusion therapy maximizes the neuroplastic window ketamine opens, turning a biochemical effect into lasting behavioral change.

Is a physician present during the entire ketamine infusion?

Yes. Dr. Truman Milling Jr., MD, FACEP is present bedside for the complete infusion. Dr. Milling is a board-certified emergency physician with 60+ peer-reviewed publications including in The New England Journal of Medicine, The Lancet, and Circulation, and research funded by the NHLBI/NIH. His full-session presence is a rare standard in the ketamine therapy market.

Why only one ketamine infusion? Other clinics offer packages of six.

Selling packages is a business model, not a medical protocol. At Lake Austin Psychotherapy, a single low-dose infusion paired with dedicated integration therapy achieves sustained relief in over 95% of clients. The integration therapy — weekly sessions in the days and weeks following the infusion — is what converts the neuroplastic window ketamine opens into permanent change. Repeated infusions without rigorous integration are less effective and more costly than one infusion done right.

What does a ketamine session feel like?

Most people experience a gentle, dreamlike shift in awareness — visual, conceptual, or both. The experience is calm; most clients describe it as peaceful rather than disorienting. The sub-anesthetic dose produces an altered state without loss of consciousness. A clinician and physician are present throughout, and the experience is processed in integration therapy sessions in the days following.

Is ketamine therapy safe?

Ketamine has been used safely in medicine since the 1960s. At sub-anesthetic doses, it produces altered awareness without loss of consciousness. Every session is physician-administered and monitored by Dr. Milling, with care tailored to your medical history. A thorough medical screening precedes every infusion.

Who is not a candidate for ketamine-assisted psychotherapy?

KAP is not appropriate for people who are pregnant or nursing, have had a recent stroke or heart attack, have a history of active psychosis or schizophrenia, or have bladder inflammation (ketamine cystitis). Dr. Milling reviews all medical history before any infusion is scheduled.

Do you provide transportation to ketamine sessions?

Yes. Private luxury transportation with a professional chauffeur is available from Austin, Dallas, Houston, and San Antonio. You cannot drive yourself to or from a ketamine infusion; the practice ensures transportation is never a barrier to care.

Is ketamine therapy the same as getting high?

No. Therapeutic KAP uses a sub-anesthetic dose calibrated for neuroplasticity and psychological processing, not recreation. The experience is a calm, dreamlike shift in awareness under full physician monitoring. The context is completely different from recreational use: a clinical setting, a physician bedside, a therapist present, and a structured therapeutic intention. The dose, the setting, and the outcome are unrelated.

What mental health conditions does ketamine-assisted psychotherapy treat?

KAP has clinical evidence for treatment-resistant depression (TRD), PTSD, treatment-resistant anxiety, prolonged grief disorder, and alcohol use disorder. It is not a first-line treatment — it is specifically indicated when standard therapy and medication have not produced adequate relief. The free consultation includes a clinical review of your history to determine whether KAP is appropriate.

How long do the effects of ketamine therapy last?

Over 95% of clients report sustained relief for months following a single infusion paired with integration therapy. Duration varies by individual, condition severity, and how consistently the integration therapy is completed. The neuroplastic window ketamine opens lasts approximately 2–4 weeks after the infusion — the integration sessions during this window are what extend relief into lasting change. Some clients return for a second infusion after 6–12 months; many do not need to.

Prolonged Grief Disorder Therapy

Can therapy help with grief, stress, and life transitions?

Yes. Talk therapy, EMDR, and ketamine-assisted therapy all address grief, chronic stress, and life transitions. EMDR is particularly effective for the rumination that often accompanies grief. For grief that has lasted over 12 months and meets clinical criteria, Lake Austin Psychotherapy offers the Columbia Prolonged Grief Disorder protocol, with a 70% efficacy rate — among the most effective grief-specific treatments available.

What is Prolonged Grief Disorder and how is it different from normal grief?

Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) is a recognized DSM-5-TR clinical diagnosis where grief becomes stuck — typically for more than 12 months — rather than gradually easing. It is characterized by intense longing, difficulty accepting the loss, a disrupted sense of identity, and an inability to re-engage with life. Around 10% of bereaved people develop PGD. Unlike normal grief, it does not improve on its own without targeted treatment.

What is the Columbia protocol for Prolonged Grief Disorder?

Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT) was developed at Columbia University’s Center for Complicated Grief by Dr. Katherine Shear. It combines CBT, interpersonal therapy, and grief-specific exposure techniques. Clinical trials show a 70% efficacy rate. Mary K. Moore trained directly with the Columbia team — not through a secondary course — and pairs the protocol with a unique physician chart review by Dr. Milling for clients who have unanswered questions about the medical circumstances of their loved one’s death.

Do I need to have experienced a recent loss to be treated?

No. The practice works with both acute and longstanding grief, including losses that occurred decades ago and were never fully processed. If grief is still significantly affecting your daily life, treatment is appropriate regardless of when the loss occurred. A free virtual grief assessment is available.

How long does grief last?

There is no single timeline. For most people, the most acute phase of grief eases gradually over the first 6–12 months, though grief rarely disappears entirely, nor should it. What typically shifts is the intensity and the ability to re-engage with life. If grief is still consuming your daily functioning after 12 months, or has never meaningfully eased, it may meet the clinical criteria for Prolonged Grief Disorder — a treatable condition. A free virtual assessment can clarify whether what you’re experiencing is typical bereavement or something that would benefit from specialized treatment.

What are the stages of grief?

The five stages model (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) was developed by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross in 1969 to describe the experience of dying patients, not bereavement. It is widely recognized but widely misapplied. Grief is not linear. Most people experience grief in waves that come and go, often triggered by anniversaries, milestones, or unexpected reminders. If you are stuck in one emotional state and cannot move through it, that is worth exploring with a clinician.

When does grief become a disorder?

Grief becomes Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD) when it persists at acute intensity for more than 12 months after the loss and significantly disrupts daily functioning. The DSM-5-TR added PGD as a formal diagnosis in 2022. Key symptoms include intense yearning, difficulty accepting the loss, a disrupted sense of identity, avoidance of reminders, and an inability to re-engage with life. Around 10% of bereaved people develop PGD. Unlike normal grief, it does not resolve on its own without targeted treatment. The Columbia University protocol used at Lake Austin Psychotherapy has a 70% efficacy rate for PGD.

Alcohol Use Disorder

Does Lake Austin Psychotherapy treat alcohol use disorder?

Yes. The practice offers a specialized outpatient protocol combining a low-dose ketamine infusion, weekly therapy, and a monthly Naltrexone injection. Naltrexone blocks alcohol’s reward pathways in the brain; the ketamine and therapy work together to form new neural patterns and address the underlying trauma often driving the disorder. For substance use without alcohol as the primary issue, trauma-informed EMDR and counseling are used without ketamine.

Can I continue my daily life during alcohol use disorder treatment?

Yes. The AUD protocol is entirely outpatient and discreet. No inpatient admission, no leave of absence required, and no disruption to your daily schedule beyond the scheduled sessions. This makes it a practical alternative to residential treatment for people who need to maintain their personal and professional lives during recovery.
