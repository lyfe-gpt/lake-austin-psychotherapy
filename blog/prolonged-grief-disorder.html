Prolonged Grief

When Does Grief Become a Disorder? Understanding Prolonged Grief

Courtney Kurtz, LPC-A November 2025 10 min read

Key takeaways

  • Prolonged grief is a DSM-5-TR diagnosis, distinct from depression and normal bereavement
  • It affects roughly 10% of bereaved people and does not resolve on its own with time
  • Evidence-based therapy can help, including EMDR and IFS for grief that has become stuck
  • The work focuses on addressing what is keeping grief frozen, not rushing the process
  • Free discovery call, flexible scheduling, telehealth throughout Texas

Grief is supposed to hurt. Nobody expects it to be easy, or fast, or linear. Most people understand that losing someone you love changes you, that the world is smaller afterward, that certain songs or dates or ordinary Tuesday mornings will carry weight for a long time.

What most people don’t know is that for roughly 10% of bereaved people, grief doesn’t follow even the broad and forgiving arc that most people experience. Instead of gradually finding a new rhythm, their grief remains as acute as it was in the earliest days, sometimes for years. Sometimes for decades.

If this sounds familiar, there’s a name for what you’re experiencing. And there are evidence-based approaches that help many people find meaningful relief.

What Prolonged Grief Actually Is

Prolonged grief (PGD), formerly known as Complicated Grief, was added to the DSM-5-TR (the diagnostic manual used by mental health clinicians) in 2022. This recognition was significant: it means that for the first time, the experience of grief that doesn’t resolve has an official clinical status, separate from depression and distinct from normal bereavement.

The defining feature of PGD is grief that persists at an acute, distressing intensity for more than 12 months following a loss (6 months per ICD-10 criteria). Not grief that comes in waves, which is normal. But grief that feels as raw and present as it did in the first weeks, even years later.

The DSM-5-TR criteria also require the presence of specific symptoms. These include:

  • Intense yearning or longing for the person who died
  • Preoccupation with thoughts or memories of the deceased
  • Intense emotional pain, including sorrow, guilt, anger, and bitterness related to the death
  • Difficulty accepting the death
  • Feeling that a part of yourself has died
  • Avoidance of reminders of the loss (or the opposite, seeking them out intensely)
  • Significant difficulty engaging in social relationships or activities
  • A sense that life is meaningless without the deceased

These symptoms must cause significant impairment in daily functioning to meet the diagnostic threshold. This isn’t about how much you miss someone. It’s about whether grief has become the organizing principle of your life.

Normal Grief vs. Prolonged Grief

The distinction matters, both for understanding your experience and for determining what kind of support is most helpful.

Normal Grief Prolonged Grief
Intense pain that gradually softens over months to a year or more Grief remains at acute intensity past 12 months with little or no softening
Comes in waves, triggered by anniversaries, reminders, or no apparent reason Daily functioning, including work, relationships, and self-care, is significantly impaired
The person is able to function, connect with others, and experience moments of joy The person feels unable to access positive memories without overwhelming pain
Gradually integrates the loss into a new sense of normal The relationship to the deceased feels unresolved, like the death isn’t fully real, or like there’s something left undone

The key distinction is not how much the person loved the deceased. It’s not a measure of the depth or quality of the relationship. PGD can develop after any significant loss, including a spouse, a child, a parent, a close friend, even a relationship that was complicated or ambivalent.

Why PGD Doesn’t Resolve on Its Own

Ordinary grief follows a process the brain knows how to do. It’s painful and slow, but the neural pathways that govern grief are doing what they’re designed to do: integrating the reality of the loss, maintaining an ongoing internal representation of the person who died, and gradually recalibrating what their absence means for daily life.

In prolonged grief, this process gets interrupted. The brain’s threat-detection system, the same system involved in PTSD, can become locked in a kind of holding pattern. The death is experienced as an ongoing threat to the self, rather than something that happened in the past. The grief doesn’t move because the brain hasn’t been able to register the loss as finished.

This is a neurobiological process, not a psychological weakness. It doesn’t respond to time or willpower the way people expect it to. And it doesn’t respond well to conventional grief support. Support groups and general therapy can reduce isolation and provide validation, but they don’t address the mechanism that’s keeping the grief stuck.

Evidence-Based Approaches to Grief Therapy

Grief therapy is structured work, not open-ended support. Where general counseling offers validation and a place to express grief, evidence-based grief therapy is designed to address the mechanism keeping grief frozen. The goal is not to rush the process but to remove what is interrupting it.

Two approaches I find particularly effective for grief that has become stuck are EMDR and IFS.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) works with how loss is stored in the nervous system. A death, especially a traumatic one, can be held as an unprocessed memory that keeps activating as though the loss is still happening. EMDR helps the brain complete what it couldn’t do on its own.

IFS (Internal Family Systems) works with the protective parts of you that are keeping grief at bay. Often, grief stays frozen because some part of you is not ready to accept the loss, or is afraid of what accepting it would mean. IFS creates space to work with those parts rather than pushing through them.

The goal is not to stop grieving. It is to help grief move into something you can carry, rather than something that carries you.

You Don’t Have to Keep Carrying This Alone

To anyone reading this who has been wondering for years why their grief isn’t getting better:

You are not doing grief wrong. You are not weaker than the people around you who seem to have moved on. You are experiencing a recognized medical condition with an evidence-based treatment and a strong likelihood of meaningful relief.

You are allowed to feel better. Not by forgetting. Not by moving on as though the person didn’t matter. But by learning to carry your love for them in a way that doesn’t require suffering as its condition.
